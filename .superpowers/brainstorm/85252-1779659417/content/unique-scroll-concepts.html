Making the scroll experience actually unique

Five concepts that use ASCII as more than decoration. Click what resonates — or tell me none of them do.

1

Erosion / Excavation

The page below the hero starts fully covered in dense ASCII (████). As you scroll, characters erode away from the center outward — dissolving from █ → ▓ → ▒ → ░ → nothing — revealing the clean content underneath. You're literally excavating information from the noise. Each section feels like uncovering something buried.

Why it's unique: Scroll becomes an act of discovery, not just navigation. The density ramp you already built runs in reverse as a reveal mechanic. Nobody does this.

2

Terminal render

Below the hero, content appears as if being printed to a terminal in real-time as you scroll into each section. Characters type in left-to-right, lines appear top-to-bottom. Section headers scramble-resolve (like ScrambleLink but for headings). The whole page feels like output from a process that's running — your scroll is the input.

Why it's unique: The metaphor is "my career is a running process." Scroll = execute. The ASCII field becomes the terminal's ambient state between outputs.

3

Characters become content

The ambient ASCII characters literally rearrange themselves to form each section's text as you scroll to it. The background noise drifts, clusters, and snaps into readable words. When you scroll away, they scatter back to noise. The background is the content — it just hasn't organized yet.

Why it's unique: There's no separation between decoration and content. The page is one living ASCII field where meaning crystallizes at the right moment. Technically hard but visually unprecedented.

4

Density as data

The ASCII field's density actually maps to your story. Dense regions (█▓▓) where you were deep in work. Flowing/sparse regions (·:·) for transitions. The field becomes an abstract timeline — a visual EKG of your career. Content sections overlay at the right positions. The breathing shifts with the narrative weight of each section.

Why it's unique: The background isn't random — it's a data visualization of you. Someone who notices this gets a dopamine hit. Everyone else just sees beautiful texture.

5

Cursor as flashlight

Content sections are obscured/dimmed by the ASCII field. Your cursor creates a clear radius that reveals the content underneath — like holding a light to a document in the dark. You explore the page physically. On mobile, content reveals on scroll position instead. The halo you already have, but for content, not just ASCII density.

Why it's unique: Turns passive reading into active exploration. The visitor has to engage to see. Risky for usability but unforgettable if pulled off.

These can combine. Erosion + Terminal. Density-as-data + Cursor-flashlight. Or none of these — tell me what sparks and I'll riff on it.
